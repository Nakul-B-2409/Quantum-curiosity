Have I Ever Touched Anything?

It may feel like you’re touching something when you hold a book, press a key, or grab an object—but at the atomic level, you never truly make contact. The electrons in your skin repel the electrons in the other object due to electromagnetic force. This repulsion creates the illusion of touch. There is always a microscopic distance between your atoms and the atoms of everything you “touch.” What you feel is actually a force field pushing back—not real contact between surfaces.

Then How Does a Pen Write?

A pen writes because of a combination of controlled pressure and fluid dynamics. Though the tip of the pen doesn’t technically touch the paper on an atomic level, the pressure forces ink molecules out, which then adhere to the paper’s surface. The ink fills tiny gaps in the paper fibers, creating visible marks. This is made possible through intermolecular attractions like adhesion and cohesion. So while the pen tip itself doesn’t “touch,” the liquid ink still transfers and binds physically with the paper structure.

Why Does Ink Stick If Electrons Repel?

Ink molecules are complex, containing polar and non-polar parts. Even though electrons repel each other, the molecules interact via van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonding, and dipole interactions. These weak attractions allow the ink molecules to stick to the cellulose fibers in paper. The repulsion is still there, but the attractive forces between the ink and paper are stronger at very close range. This explains how ink adheres even when the particles technically repel on a subatomic scale.

Why Does Friction Work If Nothing Touches?

Friction isn’t caused by solid surfaces scraping against each other, as it may seem. At the atomic level, friction is due to electromagnetic interactions between the electron clouds of atoms. These clouds push against each other, resisting motion. Microscopically, surfaces are uneven—bumps and valleys interlock, adding to resistance. So friction is the combined result of electromagnetic repulsion and mechanical interlocking. Even if atoms don’t literally touch, their force fields collide, producing the effect we call friction.

How Does Lubricant Reduce Friction?

Lubricants work by creating a thin molecular layer between two surfaces. This layer reduces direct atomic repulsion and prevents surface irregularities from interlocking. The molecules of the lubricant are usually slippery or non-polar, meaning they don’t bind tightly to either surface. This reduces resistance and allows the objects to slide more easily past one another. Lubricants also often contain additives that reduce wear, carry heat away, and protect surfaces from corrosion, making them even more efficient in lowering friction.

How Does Glue Make Things Stick?

Glue sticks by forming strong molecular bonds between itself and the surfaces it’s applied to. Depending on the glue, these bonds may be physical (like van der Waals forces) or chemical (covalent or ionic bonding). When glue dries or cures, it hardens and locks into the microscopic crevices of both surfaces. This dual action—molecular attraction and mechanical interlocking—creates strong adhesion. Even though atoms don’t “touch,” the electron interactions are enough to hold materials together with surprising strength.

Why Does My Hand Stay Wet Even Upside Down?

Water molecules stick to your skin due to cohesion and adhesion. Water is a polar molecule, which means it has a slight positive and negative end. These poles are attracted to polar substances like skin. Even when your hand is upside down, surface tension keeps water droplets clinging to you. Gravity can pull the water down, but if the droplet is small and the adhesive force to your skin is stronger, it remains attached. So molecular forces beat gravity—temporarily.

Are All Things Polar?

No, not all substances are polar. Polarity depends on the distribution of electric charges in a molecule. Polar molecules have uneven charge distribution (like water), which makes them interact strongly with other polar substances. Non-polar molecules (like oils or oxygen gas) have even distribution and don’t mix well with polar substances. Whether a material is polar or not determines things like solubility, surface tension, and how substances stick to one another. Polarity is crucial in understanding chemical behavior.

How Do Fingerprints Stay on Surfaces?

Your fingertips contain oils, sweat, and other secretions. When you touch a surface, even lightly, small amounts of these substances transfer and fill microscopic grooves on the object. These residues leave behind a pattern that matches your fingerprint ridges. Even though your atoms don’t physically touch the atoms of the object, the oils and moisture bridge the atomic gap and “stick” temporarily due to intermolecular forces. That’s why prints stay, even if touch is an illusion.
